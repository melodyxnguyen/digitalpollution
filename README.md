# Pforzheimer Honors College Thesis
## Digital Pollution: Understanding the Energy Costs of Large Language Models

In an age when our lives are increasingly digitized, the environmental costs of our digital habits often go unseen. Every email, photo, cloud file, and now interaction with large language models (LLMs) relies on physical infrastructure. Behind every query are data centers filled with servers that consume enormous amounts of electricity and water to train, store, and generate information. Scholars refer to this invisible demand for power as digital pollution.

The goal of this project is to measurably improve public understanding of sustainable digital usage and how large language models consume energy and resources, and to evaluate through surveys whether targeted education can influence users' attitudes and intended behaviors. To achieve this, the research adopts a creative approach that combines empirical analysis with an interactive educational website designed to visualize and explain ways to prevent e-waste and the energy costs associated with LLM training and inference.

The central research question guiding this study is: Do people understand the energy and emissions costs associated with large language models, and can a brief educational intervention improve that understanding and shift behavioral intentions?

To address this question, the study focuses on the following variables:

- Objective knowledge: what users actually know
- Perceived knowledge: what users believe they understand
- Attitudes: how important sustainability and transparency are to users
- Behavioral intentions: how users plan to act after learning
- Exposure: whether users interacted with the educational website (pre vs. post)

Through surveys, data analysis, and educational design, this study evaluates how awareness of LLM energy use shapes user understanding and behavior.
